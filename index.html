Original Sin

- the sin committed by Adam and Eve that affects all of humanity

Concupiscence

- the inclination to sin that all human beings are born with (like the natural desires you have for things that are not good for you)

Temptation

- an actual enticement to sin (like someone who invites you to do drugs)

Personal Sin

- a sin that a person actually commits (you must make the free decision)

Sin

- a failure to love God, your neighbor, and yourself

Venial Sin

- a sin that weakens our relationship with God and others, but does not destroy our relationship with God (charity in the heart)

Mortal Sin

- a sin thateffectively destroys our relationship with God (the life of charity)

Justification

- the gracious action of God which frees us from all sin (original and personal), makes us ‘righteous’ in God’s eyes as his adopted son or daughter, and sanctifies us

Sanctification

- Growth in holiness (that is, becoming more holy)

Salvation

- Eternal happiness with God

Free Will

- The ability to make choices in your life, either to follow God, or not

Baptism and Concupiscence

Baptism takes away Original Sin, but not concupiscence.

Why Doesn’t Baptism take away concupiscence?

Christian life involves an ongoing struggle against sin and temptation. Concupiscence represents one aspect of this struggle. While baptism initiates a person into the life of grace through justification, it doesn't mean that they are suddenly immune to the effects of sin or temptation. Instead, it marks the beginning of a lifelong journey of growth in holiness and continual conversion. Through baptism, Catholics receive God’s grace, which helps them resist sin and grow in holiness. However, overcoming concupiscence requires ongoing effort and cooperation with God's grace. It's a process of continually choosing to align one's will with God's and resisting the temptations that arise from concupiscence.
